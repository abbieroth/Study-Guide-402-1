Module 1 — Canadian Legal System

What is law

A set of rules enforced by the state, backed by courts and sanctions, distinct from ethics/morals/social norms. Purpose: maintain order, protect rights, support economic activity, resolve disputes. For business it enables contracts/commerce, allocates risk, regulates market behaviour, and creates compliance obligations.

Four sources of Canadian law

  • Common law — judge-made law based on precedent. Applies everywhere outside Quebec.
  • Civil law — Quebec only; based on the Civil Code (codified private law), less reliant on precedent.
  • Statutes & regulations — statutes are passed by legislatures; regulations are detailed rules made by government bodies under a statute (more flexible, change faster).
  • Constitution / Charter — Constitution Act 1867 (division of federal/provincial powers) and Constitution Act 1982 (includes the Charter of Rights and Freedoms). The Constitution is the supreme law of Canada. The notwithstanding clause (s. 33) lets a government temporarily override certain Charter rights.

Court hierarchy (lowest → highest)

  1. Provincial courts (small claims, most criminal matters)
  2. Superior courts (serious criminal, large civil, family)
  3. Courts of appeal
  4. Supreme Court of Canada — matters of national importance only

Courts interpret legislation, apply precedent, develop the common law, resolve disputes. Administrative tribunals (labour, human rights, securities) are specialized, less formal decision-makers subject to judicial review.

Civil vs. criminal

CivilCriminal
NaturePrivate dispute between partiesPublic offence against society
Brought byPlaintiffThe Crown / state
Standard of proofBalance of probabilities (~51%)Beyond a reasonable doubt (~92%)
RemedyDamages / ordersPunishment (fine, imprisonment)

The same conduct can trigger both. Businesses mostly face civil liability.

Litigation process (order)

Pleadings (Statement of Claim → Statement of Defence → possible counterclaim/third-party claim) → Discovery → Trial → Appeal. Litigation is expensive, slow, complex — raising access to justice concerns.

ADR (Alternative Dispute Resolution)

  • Negotiation — parties resolve directly
  • Mediation — neutral third party facilitates; non-binding
  • Arbitration — neutral third party decides; usually binding

Advantages: faster, cheaper, private, preserves relationships. Disadvantages: less predictable, limited appeal rights.

Class actions

One case for many plaintiffs sharing common legal issues, brought by representative plaintiff(s). Improves access to justice. Requires court certification; class counsel often paid on contingency; the court must approve any settlement.

Module 2 — Agency

Definition

Agency is the relationship where one person (the agent) is in law able to represent another (the principal) so as to affect the principal's legal position with third parties by making contracts or disposing of property. The principal grants the agent authority. Businesses transact through their agents.

Types of authority

Actual authority (real power actually granted):

Sub-typeWhat it isQuestion it answers
ExpressAuthority directly given, orally or in writing"What was the agent told to do?"
ImpliedNot stated but necessary/incidental to carry out express authority"What must be done to do that job?"
UsualAuthority that normally comes with a particular position/title"What does someone in this role normally do?"
CustomaryAuthority from customs/practices of a trade or industry"What does the industry expect?"
⚓ Usual = role; Customary = industry. (e.g., stockbroker / ship's master = customary.)

Ostensible / Apparent authority — authority that appears to exist based on the principal's representation to a third party, even when the agent has no actual authority. A claim by a third party against the principal. Elements:

  1. The principal made (or permitted) a representation that the agent had authority;
  2. The third party relied on that representation;
  3. The reliance was reasonable (and to their detriment).

If proven, the principal is bound even though the agent breached instructions / exceeded limits.

Duties of the agent (fiduciary)

Loyalty; act in the principal's best interest & avoid conflicts; no secret profit / full disclosure; good faith; act within authority. Also delegatus non potest delegare — a delegate cannot delegate.

Duties of the principal

Remuneration (pay), reimbursement, indemnification, cooperation, good faith.

Principal's liability for the agent's torts

"A tort is no less harmful when committed by an agent, just as a contract is no less binding when negotiated by an agent." A principal can be bound by an agent's tort if committed within the scope of authority/employment.

Cases

Thompson v. Meade (1891) — An agent (stockbroker) must act for the client, not to profit himself; cannot secretly buy low and charge the client high. Principle: an agent owes a fiduciary duty and may not take a secret profit at the principal's expense.

Lloyd v. Grace, Smith & Co. [1912] — A solicitor's managing clerk fraudulently induced a widow to transfer her property to him. Firm held vicariously liable because the fraud was within the class of work the clerk was authorized to do. Principle: a principal is liable for an agent's fraud committed within the scope of apparent authority, even where the agent acted for his own benefit.

Module 3 — Contract Law

Definition

A contract is a voluntary exchange of promises creating obligations that, if breached, can be enforced and remedied by the courts. May be oral or written, express or implied.

The 5 elements (T/F often removes one)

  1. Consensus — offer + acceptance (consensus ad idem, "meeting of the minds")
  2. Consideration — each party gives/gets something of value
  3. Capacity — legal ability to contract
  4. Legality — lawful purpose, not contrary to public policy
  5. Intention — intention to create legal relations

1. Consensus (offer & acceptance)

An offer must contain the material terms and may be made by conduct. A counteroffer rejects and replaces the original; a mere request for information does not. Acceptance must be absolute and unconditional and communicated. You can be bound without reading it (objective test — outward conduct, not inner mind).

2. Consideration

Every party must get a benefit. Can be money, goods, services, a promise, forbearance. A bare promise with no consideration is generally not enforceable.

3. Capacity

Minors — contracts generally voidable by the minor, except for "necessaries" (food, shelter). Also mental incapacity, intoxication.

4. Legality

  • Illegal contracts — illegal in their very object (unenforceable)
  • Contracts performed illegally — lawful purpose but carried out unlawfully

5. Intention

Presume intention to be bound in commercial/business relationships; presume no legal intention in domestic/social ones (both rebuttable).

Privity of contract

"When two parties enter a contract, they create a world of law unto themselves." Only the parties acquire rights/obligations; outsiders cannot enforce or be bound (subject to exceptions).

Vitiating factors

Mistake · Misunderstanding · Non est factum · Misrepresentation · Duress & undue influence · Unconscionable transactions.

  • Non est factum ("it is not my deed") — signer fundamentally mistaken about the nature of the document.
  • Misrepresentation — a false statement of fact that induces the contract.
  • Duress / undue influence — improper pressure overbearing free will.
  • Unconscionable transaction — gross inequality of bargaining power + improvident bargain.

End of the contractual relationship

Performance · Tender · Breach · Repudiation · Frustration. Tender = an offer to perform. Repudiation = a party signals it won't perform. Frustration = unforeseen event makes performance impossible/radically different, no fault of either party (arises after formation — contrast mistake, which exists at formation).

Damages

A monetary award to compensate the innocent party. Two limits: Remoteness (only losses reasonably foreseeable at contracting) and Mitigation (victim must take reasonable steps to reduce loss).

Cases

Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. [1893] — £100 offered to anyone who caught flu after using the ball as directed; £1,000 deposited to show sincerity. Principle: an advertisement can be a unilateral offer to the world; performing the conditions is acceptance, and notification of acceptance is not required. The deposit showed intention (not mere "puff").

Brogden v. Metropolitan Railway (1877) — Parties supplied/bought coal on the terms of a draft never formally signed. Principle: a contract can be formed by conduct; acting on the terms of an unexecuted draft = acceptance. (Mental assent alone isn't enough.)

Raffles v. Wichelhaus (1864) — Cotton "ex Peerless from Bombay," but two ships named Peerless sailed; each party meant a different one. Principle: mutual mistake about a fundamental term = no consensus ad idem = no contract.

Jarvis v. Swan Tours (1972) — A "house-party" ski holiday fell far short of the brochure. Principle (Lord Denning): damages can include mental distress / loss of enjoyment, especially for contracts whose purpose is pleasure.

Module 4 — Contract Drafting

Core message: bad drafting is a business risk, not just a legal one. The cost = wasted time, money, lost anticipated benefits, and disputes. Causes: over-reliance on precedents, belief that legalese is precise, "tested" language, status through obfuscation.

Common drafting problems (and fixes)

  • Over-complicated titles/terms — strip filler ("Comprehensive Tri-partite Merger Agreement" → "Merger Agreement").
  • Defined terms — capitalize and use consistently; don't mismatch.
  • "Quill pen" language — drop archaic legalese ("heretoforth," "desireth," "said assets"); plain English is shorter and clearer.
  • ALL CAPS — outdated; use bold, white space, bullets.
  • Misuse of "shall" — should impose a duty on the subject. Test: can you replace it with "has an obligation to"? If not, use "will" or present tense ("constitutes").
  • Redundancy — cut "including but not limited to," doubled phrases.
  • Ambiguity — the central theme.
🚫 NOT directly tested: the specific four types of ambiguity below. The instructor said you only need to be aware of them to identify ambiguity generally. Don't over-memorize the categories.
  • Time — specify AM/PM and time zone.
  • Date — write dates in full (avoid DD/MM vs MM/DD).
  • Party — say "both X and Y" or "either X or Y."
  • Syntactic — restructure so a modifier attaches to the right thing.

The Comma Cases

Rogers v. Bell Aliant ("million-dollar comma," CRTC 2006–07) — A second comma made a termination clause modify both clauses, so Bell could terminate on one year's notice. Rogers ultimately won on appeal — the CRTC relied on the French version of the bilingual agreement, which had only one interpretation (full five-year term). Principle: a single comma can change meaning and cost millions; in bilingual/regulatory documents both versions are read together.

Oakhurst Dairy ("Oxford comma case," Maine) — A wage exemption listed "...packing for shipment or distribution of" goods. The missing serial comma made it unclear whether that was one activity or two. Principle: omitting the serial comma creates ambiguity; courts resolve ambiguous wage statutes in favour of employees (settled ~$5M).

Module 5 — Business Organizations

Three ways to carry on business in Canada: Sole Proprietorship · Partnership · Corporation.

Sole Proprietorship

A business with no legal existence separate from its owner. Pros: simple to form/dissolve, tax simplicity. Cons: no perpetuity, unlimited personal liability, hard to raise capital.

Partnership

Definition: persons carrying on business in common with a view of profit. (A view to profit is essential.) Cons: no separate legal personality, unlimited personal liability, and liability is joint and several — each partner is liable for the totality of the partnership's debts, including obligations created by other partners.

Liability timeline (Partnerships Act):

  • Before joining — not liable for prior acts.
  • While a partner — fully liable.
  • After leaving — still liable for obligations incurred during your time (until proper notice of retirement is given).
  • "Holding-out" partners — representing yourself as a partner attracts liability without the benefits.

Partnership cases

Thorne v. New Brunswick (WCB) (1962) — A partner who worked in the mill for "wages" was injured. Principle: a partnership is not a separate legal entity, so a partner cannot be an employee of his own firm.

Ernst & Young v. Falconi (1994) — One partner used the firm to help clients defraud creditors; the innocent partner was held jointly & severally liable. Principle: partners are vicariously liable for a co-partner's wrongful acts done in the ordinary course of the firm's business, even if for an improper/fraudulent purpose.

(Also referenced: McCormick v. Fasken — an equity partner is not an "employee," reinforcing partners ≠ employees.)

Corporation

A separate legal entity created under statute. Parties: shareholders (own shares), directors (manage/board), officers (titled executives). Why incorporate: limited liability, perpetual existence, easy transfer of ownership (shares), easy to add equity, some tax advantages. Drawbacks: costly, ongoing maintenance, some tax disadvantages.

Separate legal personality means: shareholder assets aren't the company's; a shareholder can contract with, manage, be a creditor of, and be an employee of the company. Shares are transferable, supporting perpetual existence.

Corporation cases

Salomon v. Salomon & Co. (1897, HL) — Mr. Salomon incorporated his boot business and took secured debentures; on failure, creditors tried to hold him personally liable. Principle (foundational): a properly incorporated company is a legal entity distinct from its shareholders — the "corporate veil." Creditors of the company cannot pursue the shareholders.

Lee v. Lee's Air Farming Ltd. [1961] — Lee was sole governing director and also chief pilot; he died in a crash and his widow claimed workers' compensation. Principle: a corporation is a separate legal person, so one person can be both controlling director/shareholder and an employee (contrast Thorne for partnerships).

Highest-yield distinctions to nail

  • Void vs. voidable — void = no contract ever existed (a nullity); voidable = valid until the protected party elects to set it aside (e.g., minors).
  • Civil vs. criminal — parties / standard of proof (51% vs. ~92%) / remedy.
  • Mistake vs. frustration — mistake exists at formation; frustration arises after formation.
  • Usual vs. customary authority — role vs. industry.
  • Actual vs. apparent authority — real granted power vs. authority created by the principal's representation to a third party.
  • Partnership vs. corporation — no separate entity / unlimited joint-and-several liability vs. separate legal entity / limited liability (Thorne vs. Lee).
  • Illegal contract vs. contract performed illegally.

Watch the absolutes (True/False trap)

Words like "always," "never," "must," "all," "only" usually make a statement false — the law is full of exceptions. Read the qualifier before answering.
